## Abstract

Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) progresses from an initial hypercoagulable phase to a severe consumptive coagulopathy and terminal hypocoagulation. While activated neutrophils are qualitatively implicated in driving this deep hypocoagulable state, their exact mechanistic and quantitative contributions remain unresolved. Here, we develop a mechanistic ordinary differential equation model of DIC with shared biochemistry to evaluate neutrophil-mediated coagulation failure. The computational framework is trained simultaneously on in vivo temporal data from control and pharmacologically myelosuppressed animal cohorts. Through parameter identifiability and ensemble analyses, the model captures the progression from hypercoagulation to deep hypocoagulation, and its predicted severity and time course agree with the independently observed pattern of lethality. We separate direct vascular toxicity from the neutrophil-mediated contribution to the coagulation disturbances. The model predicts a biphasic therapeutic window for myelosuppressive intervention, characterized by a transition between prevention and mitigation phases. The model indicates that intervention dose and timing operate as independent thresholds rather than a continuous trade-off. By translating a qualitative immunological hypothesis into a quantitative systems biology framework, this study provides testable predictions for the timing of targeted interventions in consumptive coagulopathies.

## Introduction
Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) is a systemic acquired haemostatic disorder in which massive coagulation activation initially leads to microvascular thrombosis and consumption of coagulation factors, and subsequently—as these are depleted—to haemorrhagic diathesis. DIC is not a primary disease but a complication of shock, sepsis, trauma, burns, obstetric emergencies, and poisonings, and remains a significant contributor to mortality in these settings [Levi & ten Cate, 1999; Gando et al., 2016; Levi & Scully, 2018; Squizzato et al., 2020]. Its laboratory hallmark is a characteristic temporal sequence: an initial hypercoagulable phase, a transitional consumptive coagulopathy, and a terminal hypocoagulable phase in which coagulation may nearly cease.
Neutrophilic granulocytes are traditionally viewed as effectors of innate immunity and participants in the inflammatory response accompanying DIC. A body of experimental work argues, however, that neutrophils are not passive bystanders but active participants in the coagulopathy itself—acting through their lysosomal apparatus [Massberg et al., 2010; Engelmann & Massberg, 2013; Alhamdi & Toh, 2017]. Neutrophil serine proteases, primarily elastase and cathepsin G, are stored in azurophilic (primary) granules—the characteristic reservoir of acid hydrolases, for which acid phosphatase serves as a marker [Borregaard & Cowland, 1997]. Upon degranulation, these enzymes are released into the circulation and act on haemostasis through several parallel channels. First, they enhance coagulation: elastase cleaves tissue factor pathway inhibitor (TFPI), relieving inhibition of the tissue-factor- and factor-XII-dependent cascade [Massberg et al., 2010], while neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) and nucleosomes activate factor XII via a negatively charged surface [Massberg et al., 2010; Alhamdi & Toh, 2017]. Conversely, these same proteases degrade coagulation factors: elastase cleaves fibrinogen into low-molecular-weight fragments [Plow & Edgington, 1975; Bach-Gansmo et al., 1994] and, by limited proteolysis, activates plasma factor XIII into a truncated form that is rapidly consumed in the cascade [Henriksson et al., 1980; Bagoly et al., 2008]. This bidirectional influence places neutrophil degranulation mechanistically upstream of the deep consumptive and hypocoagulable phase, consistent with the modern reframing of DIC as a manifestation of immunothrombosis, in which coagulation and innate immunity are mutually coupled [Engelmann & Massberg, 2013].
The animal model underlying this study directly tests this hypothesis. DIC is induced in rabbits with a standardised inducer (preparation "Efa-2" based on ethylphenacin—a first-generation vitamin K antagonist from the 1,3-indanedione family of anticoagulant rodenticides [Watt et al., 2005]; ≈1.25 mg/kg ethylphenacin orally), which reproduces the progression from hypercoagulation (days 1–4) through a transitional consumptive phase to deep hypocoagulation (approximately days 6–11), resolving by days 19–20 [Boiarchuk, 1998a; Boiarchuk, 1998b]. Two groups are compared: a control DIC group (Group I) and a group in which granulopoiesis is pharmacologically suppressed by the alkylating agent busulfan prior to induction (Group II) [Boiarchuk, 1998a; Boiarchuk, 2014].
A central experimental finding from this model is that pharmacological suppression of the neutrophil lineage largely prevents the deep hypocoagulable phase. In the myelosuppressed cohort, the clinically significant stage of DIC is shortened from ~14–15 to ~6 days, the hypercoagulable effect lasts only about 2 days and is 4–7 times less pronounced, and hypocoagulable shifts barely develop [Boiarchuk, 2023a]. Peak acid phosphatase activity is approximately 2.5-fold lower [Boiarchuk, 2012; Boiarchuk, 2013; Boiarchuk, 2014], and the 30% lethality observed in Group I at the peak of hypocoagulation [Boiarchuk, 1998a] is virtually absent. Together, these observations provide a causal, rather than merely correlational, argument in favour of a pathogenetic role for activated neutrophils in the consumptive phase of DIC [Boiarchuk, 2008; Boiarchuk, 2023b].
This causal picture, however, leaves two quantitative questions unresolved. First, the observed coagulation disturbances—such as prolonged recalcification time, fibrinogen depletion, and factor XIII loss—reflect a combination of direct, inducer-mediated vascular toxicity and indirect, neutrophil-mediated effects. The relative contribution of each pathway remains unquantified. Second, while busulfan strongly attenuates DIC, the exact nature of this protective effect—whether driven strictly by a reduced circulating neutrophil count, modified reaction kinetics, or a combination of both—requires formal decomposition.
Here, we address these gaps by developing a mechanistic ODE model of neutrophil-mediated DIC. We construct a shared biochemical framework and fit it simultaneously to temporal haemostatic and immunological observables from both control and myelosuppressed cohorts. To ensure robustness, we rigorously map the parameter space, distinguishing predictive variables from structurally "sloppy" dimensions typical of systems biology models. Using structurally informed priors, we then separate the direct vascular and neutrophil-mediated contributions to the coagulation cascade. We corroborate the model by demonstrating that its predicted severity and time course agree with the independently observed pattern of lethality, while accurately capturing the in-sample nadir amplitudes and peak timings. Finally, applying this corroborated framework, we predict the dynamics of myelosuppressive intervention, identifying a biphasic therapeutic window and indicating that intervention dose and timing act as independent thresholds in mitigating consumptive coagulopathy.

## Results
### Simultaneous model fitting captures divergent coagulopathy dynamics
When fitted simultaneously to both experimental cohorts, the mechanistic model captures the measured dynamics across all six observables. For the control DIC cohort (Group I, 16 sampling time points over 19 days), the mean coefficient of determination is $R^2 = 0.83$. For the busulfan-suppressed cohort (Group II, 9 points over 8 days), the model achieves a mean $R^2 = 0.69$.
The model successfully reproduces the deep hypocoagulable phase, matching the late-phase nadirs of fibrinogen and factor XIII to within 11% and 1%, respectively (Figure 1c, d). A systematic underestimation of the early hypercoagulable amplitude is confined to the vascular-mediated channels (recalcification time and fibrinogen), where the fibrinogen channel exhibits a non-monotonic intermediate profile arising from near-mutual cancellation of large opposing terms (Figure 1c). This structural penalty is accepted in exchange for a single, shared mechanistic parametrisation across both groups.
### Identifiability analysis outlines parameter constraints
Profile likelihood analysis reveals that six of the twenty-six model parameters are well-identified based on a relative profile depth threshold of $>5\%$ ($t_{p2}$, $k_m$, $t_m$, $k_d$, $s_2$, and $a_t$). The remaining twenty parameters are weakly identified, sloppy, or grid-truncated (full per-parameter profile-likelihood profiles and classification in Supplementary Appendix §S3). This sensitivity pattern represents a structural property of over-parameterised systems-biology models rather than an issue with the numerical fit. Predictions depending on the well-identified parameters—such as the inducer impulse timing and the busulfan kinetic modifiers—remain robust, although the modifiers $k_m$ and $t_m$ exhibit a more moderate profile depth (§S3.2) and consequently display correspondingly wider confidence intervals.

Because both cohorts enter DIC from comparable haemostatic states, the day-1 between-group contrast isolates the early neutrophil contribution. The structural analysis demonstrates that contact-phase disturbances (recalcification time) are predominantly driven by direct vascular toxicity. Conversely, the early alterations in factor XIII are primarily neutrophil-mediated, consistent with elastase-driven activation and subsequent consumption. For the fibrinogen channel, the model assigns a minimal neutrophil contribution (~0.05) when unconstrained; thus, the fitted fraction of 0.76 reflects the imposed day-1 prior rather than an independent feature derived from day-2 dynamics.
### Model-predicted coagulopathy severity aligns with independent lethality profiles
To corroborate the biological relevance of the framework against unmeasured physiological outcomes, the model was evaluated against the independently observed lethality contrast (30% mortality in Group I occurring strictly between days 10–12; 0% mortality in Group II). Simulating unsuppressed Group I dynamics through the bootstrap ensemble demonstrates a pronounced escalation in coagulopathy severity compared to the myelosuppressed baseline. The model predicts an approximately 21-fold separation in peak hypocoagulation between the cohorts (147 s vs. 7 s; the unrounded median ratio is 22×) (Table 3).

### Virtual experiments predict a biphasic therapeutic window and independent thresholds
#### Dose–response dependence
Holding the Group II neutrophil profile fixed, activating the fitted kinetic modifiers reduces peak hypocoagulation approximately 6-fold relative to the no-kinetic-effect case (§S6.5), while the integrated coagulation disturbance falls approximately 2.5-fold; across the full $k_m$ range, the dose-response surface shows a corresponding $\approx$7-fold corner reduction (Figure 4a, Figure 5). Decomposing this protective effect reveals that the absolute reduction in circulating neutrophil count and the modification of reaction kinetics function as pathways of comparable magnitude, yielding a 3.4-fold and 3.8-fold reduction in isolation, respectively. Their combined implementation drives the full 21-fold attenuation due to positive nonlinear interactions mediated by the effective coagulation load $g_{Hn}$ (Figure 4c; full dose–response phase diagrams and severity-metric definitions in Supplementary Appendix §S6).
#### Biphasic therapeutic window
Varying the onset of myelosuppression ($t_{\text{intervention}}$) reveals a distinct, biphasic therapeutic window (Figure 6). Intervention initiated within the first 2.5 days achieves near-complete attenuation of the coagulopathy, maintaining peak hypocoagulation shifts at 7–8 s. A transition phase occurs between days 2.5 and 3, over which the median peak severity approximately doubles (7.9 s to 14.4 s), though the absolute 95% uncertainty bands overlap. Delayed intervention after day 3 provides only partial mitigation—limiting peak severity 2- to 3-fold relative to the untreated reference—but cannot prevent acute cascade activation. This establishes an early prevention phase (0–2.5 days) and a late mitigation phase (2.5–6+ days). The full two-segment timing protocol and per-scenario temporal metrics are given in Supplementary Appendix §S5.
#### Dose $\times$ time thresholds
Simulating contrasting intervention combinations indicates that dose and timing operate as independent thresholds rather than a continuous trade-off (Table 4).

Doubling the suppression dose during the mitigation phase ($t = 4$ days) provides no measurable benefit over the baseline protocol, indicating a firm dose ceiling once the consumptive cascade is established. Conversely, halving the dose even at optimal timing ($t = 0$ days) causes therapeutic failure, resulting in a severe coagulopathy (34.0 s) that exceeds late full-dose intervention. Successful intervention thus necessitates crossing both independent barriers simultaneously.

## Discussion
### Integration of modeling architecture and predictive outputs
This study presents a mechanistic ODE model designed to evaluate the quantitative contributions of activated neutrophils to the pathogenesis of disseminated intravascular coagulation. Enforcing a shared biochemical parametrisation across divergent experimental cohorts allows the framework to capture the progression from hypercoagulation to deep hypocoagulation within a single mathematical structure. Rather than treating the control and myelosuppressed dynamics as parallel statistical phenomena, the joint fitting architecture isolates the systemic effects of granulopoiesis suppression through group-specific modifiers of reaction kinetics and impulse timing. The parameter landscape exhibits a classical sensitivity distribution, where a subset of well-identified parameters constrains the temporal dynamics and intervention thresholds, while the remaining dimensions display structural sloppiness typical of systems-biology frameworks. Crucially, the model transitions from in-sample reproduction of haemostatic nadirs to a macroscopic corroboration against independent survival profiles, as the modeled coagulopathy peak and severity separation align with the independently observed pattern of empirical lethality.
### Biological plausibility and constraints of the mechanism decomposition
The mechanism decomposition achieved under structurally informed priors provides a biologically interpretable map of early DIC activation pathways. The dominance of direct vascular toxicity in driving contact-phase disturbances (recalcification time) is consistent with the rapid induction of endothelial injury before significant neutrophil degranulation occurs. Conversely, the finding that early alterations in factor XIII are predominantly neutrophil-mediated aligns with the established molecular pathway of elastase-mediated plasma factor XIII activation. Neutrophil elastase, released from azurophilic granules, cleaves plasma factor XIII into an active, truncated form that undergoes rapid systemic consumption. This explicit kinetic link bypasses the need to assume intracellular factor XIII storage within the neutrophil compartment, consistent with current literature localising cellular factor XIII-A primarily to platelets, monocytes, and macrophages [Mitchell & Mutch, 2019; Muszbek et al., 2011].
However, this mechanism decomposition operates under specific structural boundaries that must be explicitly acknowledged. While the vascular dominance in recalcification and the neutrophil dominance in factor XIII are directionally supported and remain robust even upon removal of the prior (§S8), the neutrophil-attributed fraction for the fibrinogen channel is strictly an imposed target derived from day-1 experimental contrasts. Unconstrained optimization assigns a minimal neutrophil contribution to early fibrinogen dynamics, meaning that the assigned fraction represents a regularising prior rather than an emergent feature derived independently from day-2 temporal changes. Furthermore, this early contribution governs the initial acute-phase rise in fibrinogen, whereas the subsequent late-phase depletion (fibrinogenolysis proper) is structurally mediated by a distinct consumption term coupled to the effective coagulation load.
### Interventional dynamics and the nature of the therapeutic window
The prediction of a biphasic therapeutic window carries direct implications for the timing and design of myelosuppressive interventions in consumptive coagulopathies. In the simulated landscape, early intervention within the prevention phase limits the accumulation of effective coagulation load, effectively halting the self-sustaining acceleration of the consumptive cascade. Once this threshold is breached during the transition phase, the framework enters a mitigation mode where even maximum kinetic suppression can only attenuate, but not prevent, acute cascade activation. The observed overlap in the 95% uncertainty bands across the day 2.5 to 3 transition confirms that while the boundary is distinct in the ensemble median, it is not discretely resolved in absolute parameter space.
Decomposing the protective effect of busulfan challenges the informal assumption that protection is mediated exclusively by a reduction in circulating cell counts. The $2 \times 2$ virtual design demonstrates that the absolute count reduction and the modification of reaction kinetics operate as pathways of comparable magnitude. Neither route dominates in isolation; rather, their concurrent execution drives full therapeutic attenuation through a positive interaction, where the non-linear structure of the effective coagulation load amplifies the effect of whichever route is established second. When evaluating these inputs, a clear distinction emerges regarding their biological mapping: the count reduction directly reflects the targeted myelosuppressive action of busulfan on granulopoiesis, whereas the group-specific kinetic modifiers function as phenomenological descriptors of the altered systemic response within the depleted compartment.
Given that the experimental model utilizes a vitamin K antagonist as the primary inducer, the clinical management of such exposure [Schulman & Furie, 2015] invites a cautious parallel regarding the critical importance of intervention timing relative to coagulopathy onset. However, this comparison is limited by the phenomenological abstraction of the inducer toxicity impulse, which does not explicitly simulate the vitamin K epoxide reductase cycle. The prolonged tissue half-lives characteristic of indanedione rodenticides and the fundamental distinction between suppressing an immune-mediated consumption pathway and directly replacing depleted coagulation factors further qualify any clinical generalisation.
### Characterised methodological and data-driven limitations
To ensure rigorous evaluation of the framework, several structural and data-driven limitations must be systematically detailed:
    • Early-Phase Underestimation: The model consistently underestimates the amplitude of early hypercoagulable shifts in the vascular-mediated channels. For the fibrinogen channel, this stems from a catastrophic cancellation mode, where the final observable represents a small difference between two much larger opposing terms—representing neutrophil-driven acute-phase production and cascade consumption—making the intermediate trajectory highly sensitive to minor phase mismatches.
    • Architectural Constraint Penalty: Enforcing a shared 24-parameter core introduces an architectural penalty, resulting in a lower mean $R^2$ for the myelosuppressed cohort than could be achieved via independent fitting. This penalty is concentrated almost entirely in the structurally under-determined factor XIII channel rather than distributed uniformly—across the other five observables the joint and separate fits are comparable—and represents the formal cost of maintaining a single, consistent biochemical parametrisation across both experimental states (per-observable joint-versus-separate comparison in Supplementary Appendix §S10).
    • Structural Under-determination: The factor XIII channel in the myelosuppressed group is structurally under-determined under the available data density. This yields a broad, right-skewed bootstrap distribution where alternative parametric basins achieve stable total costs despite diverging local fit qualities—a property that is robust to the choice of residual normalisation.
    • Pharmacokinetic Abstractions: The virtual experiments idealise the myelosuppressive intervention as an instantaneous kinetic switch. In an experimental or clinical setting, the pharmacological action of an alkylating agent develops dynamically over several days, meaning the simulated timelines represent an idealised immediate exposure profile rather than a realistic pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic model.
    • Extrapolation Horizons: Due to the termination of the empirical Group II monitoring window, the model assumes a flat extrapolation of the suppressed neutrophil profile beyond day 8. While consistent with the empirical observation of cohort survival and prolonged cytopenia, this remains a structural assumption rather than a data-derived trajectory. Finally, as a veterinary framework evaluated in a rabbit model, all predicted absolute timelines and threshold magnitudes remain subject to the standard constraints of interspecies translation.
### Targeted perspectives for experimental validation
The model formulates three explicit, testable hypotheses designed for prospective experimental verification:
    1. Biphasic Window Transition: An interventional series varying the onset of myelosuppression should reveal a distinct transition in DIC severity, where the median peak hypocoagulation doubles when intervention is delayed from day 2.5 to day 3.
    2. Threshold Independence: Escalating the suppression dose beyond the established rate ceiling during the mitigation phase ($t = 4$ days) will provide no measurable improvement in coagulopathy markers, whereas reducing the dose below the required floor during the prevention phase ($t = 0$ days) will cause severe therapeutic failure despite optimal timing.
    3. Multi-Route Requirement: An alternative myelosuppressive agent that replicates the absolute neutrophil count reduction of the busulfan protocol, but fails to trigger the corresponding shift in reaction kinetics, is predicted to be only partially protective—achieving a localized reduction in peak severity of approximately 3.4-fold, falling significantly short of the full 21-fold attenuation predicted under combined pathways.

## Methods
### Experimental data and protocols
Animals and design. The present study is a retrospective computational analysis of archival in vivo data [Boiarchuk, 1998a; Boiarchuk, 2014]. The original experiments were performed on 80 sexually mature rabbits of both sexes (40 per group) with a body weight of 2.5–3.0 kg. Animals were housed in standard vivarium conditions in accordance with the bioethical principles for laboratory animals, and the experimental protocols were approved by the relevant institutional ethical committee at the time. Two groups were studied. In Group I (control DIC), disseminated intravascular coagulation was modelled with a single oral dose of the commercial inducer preparation "Efa-2" (delivering approximately $1.25$ mg/kg of the active ingredient ethylphenacin, administered via $8,330$ mg/kg of the 0.015% ready bait on an empty stomach). Mechanistically, ethylphenacin inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase, depleting vitamin K-dependent coagulation factors (II, VII, IX, X) and proteins C and S, and damaging the vascular endothelium, thereby triggering secondary DIC that progresses from hypercoagulation to deep hypocoagulation. In Group II (suppressed granulopoiesis), the same inducer dose was administered following pharmacological suppression of granulopoiesis.
Granulopoiesis suppression (Group II). Suppression was achieved with oral busulfan (1,4-butanediol dimethanesulfonate)—an alkylating agent that at low doses selectively suppresses granulopoiesis [IARC, 2012]—administered according to a two-stage regimen [Boiarchuk, 1998a; Boiarchuk, 2014]. In the first stage, busulfan was given at 10 mg/day for 5–7 days—until the absolute peripheral blood neutrophil count fell by 40–50% from the intact level. In the second stage, a maintenance dose of 4 mg/day was given for approximately 8 days to stabilise the reduced neutrophil count without deepening the cytopenia. The inducer was administered immediately thereafter. This protocol established a distinct pre-induction state, designated "M" (post-busulfan), in which the absolute neutrophil count was reduced (from ~8.0 to ~3.9 × 10⁹/L), while haemostatic parameters were modestly but significantly shifted from the intact baseline; consequently, all changes in Group II are measured relative to state M, not to the intact baseline [Boiarchuk, 2014].
Observables and measurements. Six observables were measured. Four characterise the haemostatic system: plasma recalcification time (s), thrombin time (s), fibrinogen (mg%), and factor XIII activity (%). Two characterise the neutrophil compartment: serum acid phosphatase activity (lysosomal enzyme marker) and the degranulation index (the fraction of neutrophils with fewer than ten lysosomal granules) [Pigarevsky, 1975]. Group I was sampled at 16 time points over 19 days; Group II—at 9 time points over 8 days, after which its dynamics effectively stabilise. Full provenance is documented in Supplementary Appendix §S7.
### Mathematical model
The model is a system of ODEs with five states tracking degranulation ($D$), acid phosphatase activity ($AP$), an inducer-derived coagulation pool ($H_c$), a neutrophil-derived coagulation pool ($H_n$), and factor XIII activity ($X$). $H_c$ and $H_n$ represent the vascular-mediated and neutrophil-mediated pools of consumed coagulation activity, respectively. The neutrophil-mediated effect entering the observable equations is the effective coagulation load $g_{Hn} = H_n / (1 - H_n/H_m)$, a nonlinear function reflecting accelerated consumption as $H_n$ approaches its limiting capacity $H_m$. The measured neutrophil count $N(t)$ is supplied as an experimental input interpolator for each group.
Six observables are computed as algebraic functions of the state vector. The model contains 26 parameters representing rate constants, inducer toxicity impulse kinetics, and contribution coefficients. Group I and Group II share all parameters except two group-specific modifiers ($k_m$, $t_m$) that scale the effective rate constants in Group II to represent the busulfan effect. The reduction in neutrophil count is a directly supplied experimental observation; beyond it, the model represents the group difference strictly as a change in population reaction kinetics and impulse timing ($k_m$, $t_m$) rather than modifying per-cell production rates. Rather than a mere representational choice, this architecture mathematically encodes the systems-biology null hypothesis that busulfan modifies the systemic kinetics and timing of the neutrophil compartment without altering its fundamental intracellular biochemistry.
The model contains two structural priors. The first ($W_{\text{SPLIT}} = 2.0$) fixes the neutrophil-attributed fractions of three channels on day 2 to values derived independently from the day-1 contrast between the two groups. The second ($c_x \leq 600$) constrains the factor XIII channel to a biologically interpretable subspace. Both priors were validated post-hoc by improved Group II fit relative to unconstrained alternatives: the $W_{\text{SPLIT}}$ weight sensitivity is characterised in Supplementary Appendix §S8, and the $c_x$ bound justification in §S9. The full ODE formulation, parameter definitions, and observable equations are detailed in Supplementary Appendix §S1.
### Parameter estimation
The model is fitted by minimising a joint cost function—a weighted sum of squared residuals across all six observables in both groups—using a multi-seed differential evolution pipeline with local refinement. Differential evolution (scipy.optimize.differential_evolution) is launched independently from five starting points (seeds); each global solution is refined by a Nelder-Mead → Powell → Nelder-Mead sequence (scipy.optimize.minimize). The result with the lowest cost among the five seeds is retained as the joint baseline. Full hyperparameters for the base pipeline are provided in §S2.2, for the ensemble pipeline in §S2.3; full estimates for all 26 parameters are in §S2.5.
### Robustness analysis
Model robustness is assessed via four complementary protocols: profile likelihood, parametric bootstrap ($N = 100$), leave-one-out cross-validation, and neutrophil-count perturbation. Three ensemble-based analyses share a common lightweight re-fitting pipeline (§S2.3).
Profile likelihood analysis [Raue et al., 2009; Kreutz et al., 2013] systematically evaluates the parameter landscape, classifying each parameter based on its constraint by the data. Six parameters are classified as well-identified (relative profile depth > 5%: $t_{p2}$, $k_m$, $t_m$, $k_d$, $s_2$, $a_t$), five as weakly identified, twelve as practically non-identifiable (sloppy; relative depth < 2%), and three as grid-truncated (baseline near a parameter bound), together accounting for all twenty-six parameters. This sloppiness is a structural property typical of mechanistic systems-biology models of this scale, indicating an over-parameterised landscape where certain parameter combinations compensate for one another, rather than representing a defect of the fitting process [Gutenkunst et al., 2007]. Parametric bootstrap [Efron & Tibshirani, 1993] generates synthetic datasets from baseline predictions with Gaussian noise to construct confidence intervals. Leave-one-out cross-validation [Stone, 1974] systematically removes observables to compute the PRESS statistic, while sensitivity to the neutrophil input $N(t)$ is tested via deterministic and stochastic perturbations.
### Virtual experiments
Three virtual experiments propagate parameter uncertainty by running the 100-member bootstrap ensemble through prediction tasks. In the dose–response experiment, the Group II effective rate constants are redefined on a $15 \times 15$ log-uniform grid in $(k_m, t_m)$, with the Group II neutrophil profile preserved throughout (22,500 simulations in total). In the timing experiment, the busulfan effect is switched on at a variable time $t_{\text{intervention}}$ via two-segment ODE integration across nine timing scenarios. In the dose × time experiment, scenarios testing high dose at late timing and half-dose at early timing evaluate the interaction of these thresholds. Severity and temporal metrics are evaluated on extended time scales, with the time to peak defined as the first local maximum of $g_{Hn}(t)$ to avoid finite-horizon boundary artefacts.

The experimental dataset (group time series with standard errors), together with full per-value provenance, is available in the project repository at https://github.com/SubstanceNet/boiarchuk-neutrophil-dic-model and permanently archived at Zenodo (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20673521). The primary source for all measured values is the Boiarchuk (1998) dissertation [Boiarchuk, 1998a]; see `data/csv/PROVENANCE.md` and Supplementary Information S7 for the provenance of each value. The data files are released under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International licence (CC-BY-4.0).
### Code availability
The model source code, analysis scripts, result artefacts, and a documented reproduction protocol are available in the project repository at https://github.com/SubstanceNet/boiarchuk-neutrophil-dic-model (release v1.0.0) and permanently archived at Zenodo (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20673521). Reproduction instructions are given in Supplementary Information S7 and `docs/reproduction_protocol.md`. The code is released under the Apache License 2.0. (Note: the granulopoiesis suppressant busulfan is referred to by its synonym “myelosan” in some code comments and data files.)

### Competing interests
The authors declare no financial or non-financial competing interests.

### Funding
The authors received no external funding; the work was carried out at personal expense.

### Ethics approval
This study is a retrospective computational analysis of archival in vivo data; no new animal experiments were performed. The original experiments [Boiarchuk, 1998a; Boiarchuk, 2014] were conducted on rabbits housed in accordance with the bioethical principles for laboratory animals, and the protocols were approved by the relevant institutional ethics committee at the time (see Methods).

### Author contributions
O.D.B. developed and conducted the experimental work, provided biological interpretation, and led the Introduction and Discussion. O.O. developed the mathematical model, performed numerical estimation, robustness analyses, and virtual experiments, implemented the analytical code, and led the Methods and Results. Both authors reviewed and approved the manuscript.
